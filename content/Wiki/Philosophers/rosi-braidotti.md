## Biography Summary

<div className="dropcap">

Rosi Braidotti (born 1954 in Latisana, Italy) is a contemporary continental philosopher best known for her contributions to feminist theory and posthumanist philosophy. She grew up in Australia after her family emigrated there and later pursued higher education in Europe. Braidotti completed her PhD at the **Sorbonne (Université Paris)** in 1981, where she was strongly influenced by French continental thinkers, particularly **[[Gilles Deleuze]]** and **[[Michel Foucault]]**. She later became Professor of Humanities at **Utrecht University** in the Netherlands and founded the Centre for the Humanities there.

Braidotti's work emerged in the context of late twentieth-century feminist theory and post-structuralism. She sought to move beyond both classical humanism and certain strands of postmodern relativism. Drawing on **[[Baruch Spinoza]]**, Deleuzian philosophy, feminist epistemology, and science studies, she developed a philosophical framework that addresses contemporary transformations in technology, ecology, globalization, and identity. Her work is often associated with the development of critical posthumanism and new materialist thought.

## Key Philosophical Contributions

### Nomadic Subjectivity
One of Braidotti’s earliest and most influential ideas is the concept of **nomadic subjectivity**, introduced most prominently in *Nomadic Subjects* (1994). The concept challenges the traditional philosophical idea of a unified, stable, autonomous subject—the model often associated with Enlightenment humanism.

Instead, Braidotti proposes a **dynamic, relational subject** that is constituted through shifting social, political, and historical forces. "Nomadic" does not refer literally to geographic movement but to the capacity of identity to move across established boundaries (gender, nationality, class, species, etc.). The nomadic subject resists fixed identity categories while still remaining politically engaged. For Braidotti, this model allows feminist theory to avoid both essentialism (fixed identities) and relativism (loss of political grounding).

### Posthumanism
Braidotti is one of the central theorists of **critical posthumanism**, especially through her book *The Posthuman* (2013). She argues that the classical figure of "Man" in humanist philosophy—rational, autonomous, Western, male—is historically exclusionary and no longer adequate in an age defined by biotechnology, artificial intelligence, ecological crisis, and globalization.

Her posthumanism does not reject humanity but **reconfigures subjectivity as a network of human and nonhuman relations**. Drawing heavily on **Spinoza’s monism** and **Delezean vitalism**, Braidotti frames life as a continuum of interconnected forces. The posthuman subject is therefore embedded within technological systems, ecological processes, and animal life. Her goal is to produce a philosophical framework capable of addressing contemporary ethical challenges such as climate change, biotechnology, and species relations.

### Affirmative Ethics
Braidotti also develops an **affirmative ethics**, which draws from Spinoza’s notion of *conatus* and Deleuze’s philosophy of life. Rather than focusing primarily on critique or negation, affirmative ethics emphasizes the **productive capacities of life** and the possibility of creating new forms of collective existence.

This ethical framework rejects nihilism and pessimism often associated with postmodern thought. Instead, Braidotti argues for an ethics grounded in **collective empowerment, relationality, and responsibility toward both human and nonhuman others**. The aim is to cultivate forms of subjectivity capable of responding creatively to the transformations of the Anthropocene.

## PhilPapers Survey Profile
*Based on the general orientation of her philosophy rather than explicit survey answers.*

* **Mind:** Non-reductive monism / posthuman relational ontology (strongly anti-Cartesian)
* **Free Will:** Compatibilist or relational agency (agency emerges from networks of forces rather than individual autonomy)
* **Knowledge:** Post-empiricist / situated knowledge (influenced by feminist epistemology and science studies)
* **Meta-ethics:** Ethical naturalism with Spinozist monism (life-affirming ethics rather than classical realism/anti-realism dichotomy)
* **God:** Non-theistic Spinozist immanence (often interpreted as philosophical naturalism)
* **Human Nature:** Anti-essentialist; the human is historically constructed and increasingly posthuman

## Major Works
1. *Nomadic Subjects: Embodiment and Sexual Difference in Contemporary Feminist Theory* (1994) – Introduces the concept of nomadic subjectivity and rethinks feminist subjectivity through continental philosophy.
2. *Metamorphoses: Towards a Materialist Theory of Becoming* (2002) – Develops a Deleuzian feminist ontology centered on becoming and transformation.
3. *Transpositions: On Nomadic Ethics* (2006) – Explores ethical implications of nomadic subjectivity and relational ontology.
4. *The Posthuman* (2013) – One of the foundational texts of contemporary posthumanist philosophy.
5. *Posthuman Knowledge* (2019) – Extends posthumanist theory into epistemology and the structure of knowledge production.

## Influence & Legacy
* **Influenced by:** Baruch Spinoza, Gilles Deleuze, Michel Foucault, Luce Irigaray, Donna Haraway
* **Followers/School:** Posthumanism, New Materialism, Contemporary Feminist Theory
* **Notable Critics:** Martha Nussbaum, critical humanists, some analytic philosophers skeptical of posthumanism
* **Impact on Philosophy:** Braidotti helped consolidate **posthumanism as a major philosophical movement**, bridging feminist theory, continental metaphysics, and science and technology studies. Her work has influenced debates on artificial intelligence, ecological philosophy, feminist ethics, and the conceptual future of the human in the Anthropocene.
